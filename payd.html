Payd

Your all inclusive e-wallet application

Problem

Online sales and transactions have steadily increased over the last decade. The COVID pandemic had increased this rate even further, resulting in more and more apps designed to manage finances.

This led to the question: why do users need to handle so many different apps in order to understand their finances?

ROLE
UX Researcher
UX/UI Designer

TEAM
Solo
UX Bootcamp

TIMELINE
June 2021
October 2021

Analysis

I analyzed three popular finance management apps and found that though each app performs specific functions well, just one was not enough for users to understand their entire financial summary.



User Interviews

I conducted user interviews to understand what three finance app users valued most in their apps. My research goals were to gauge vital features, contexts of use, and pain points surrounding competitor apps.

Synthesis

I personified my research data to help me narrow who I was designing for. I also shaped journey maps to illustrate relatable scenarios and product use cases for each persona. The personas and journey maps helped me develop three key insights.

Insights

Design

Taking my three key insights, I created three core user flows to focus on as I sketched lofi wireframes. These sketches acted as pseudo-MVPs that put functionality in the forefront. I eventually expanded these sketches to a hifi prototype ready for iteration and improvement.

Iteration

I observed how six finance app users navigated through my prototype through usability testing. I organized trends using affinity maps and a rainbow spreadsheet to prioritize usability errors. I then continuously iterated my designs based on this feedback as well as visual and accessibility guidelines.

Solution

Payd provides a comprehensive financial overview of all its users' expenses by rolling multiple finance apps into one neat bundle and being a payment method itself.

Lessons

  1. I learned the difference between research and interview questions. I now know the importance of keeping interview questions open ended to effectively gather qualitative information.
  2. I had a habit of tunnel visioning. I should have prioritized functionality and feedback-based changes over minor visual details to keep from being overwhelmed during iteration.
  3. This being my first full UX/UI project, I gained a better understanding of what processes to use, when to use them, and how they deliver insights that affect the big picture.
